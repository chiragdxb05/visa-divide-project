The Global Mobility Divide:
Why Your Passport Determines Your Freedom

Between 1969 and 2010, globalisation reshaped the world, but the freedom to cross borders became more unequal, not less. OECD nations soared. Africa fell behind. The passport you hold at birth still determines where you can go.

Chirag Ramesh · 244897 Hertie School March 2026

A Legacy Written in Borders

The global mobility regime was shaped as much by empire as by geography. The same colonial order that drew borders across Africa also helped produce the hierarchies of belonging that still shape who moves freely and who does not.

That legacy persists in the visa system, where nationality remains one of the strongest predictors of mobility. As Mau et al. (2015) show, visa-free travel did not become more equal between 1969 and 2010. Instead, the gap widened between a mobile global North and a constrained global South.

"Colonial borders continue to inform strangeness and negative othering, determining who is a trusted traveller and who is not." Sanni, J.S. (2020). Decolonising Borders. Theoria.

The Visa as Instrument of Control

Visas were once presented as temporary tools of wartime surveillance. Instead, they became permanent instruments for controlling movement.

Today, they operate as a form of “remote control,” allowing wealthy states to filter travellers before journeys even begin. The process is opaque, appeals are weak, and nationality remains a key predictor of rejection.

But nationality is not a measure of individual risk. It often acts as a proxy for race, class, and colonial history. Senegal illustrates the gap: rejection rates exceed 46%, while only 3.6% of Senegalese nationals are found to be illegally residing in the EU after entry.

"The visa process has become not a security measure but a profitable barrier, generating €60 million in non-refundable fees from African applicants for rejected applications in 2024 alone." Henley & Partners Global Mobility Report, 2024

A Path Forward

Bridging this divide requires differentiated responsibilities and targeted action by both “winner” and “loser” states, supported by international institutions.

01
For the EU & Winner States
End nationality-based profiling in visa decisions

Visa decisions must be based on individual assessments, professional ties, travel purpose, and financial circumstance, not on the blanket proxy of nationality. The data does not support high rejection rates: Senegalese rejection rates exceed 46%, yet only 3.6% are found illegally residing in the EU.

02
For the EU & Winner States
Implement a two-stage, transparent fee structure

Replace the current system with a two-stage process: a lower fee covers initial document verification; only upon passing does an applicant pay for substantive assessment.

03
For the African Union
Strengthen pan-African mobility first

Accelerate implementation of the AfCFTA protocols on free movement of people.

04
For the African Union
Negotiate collectively, not bilaterally

The AU must leverage its collective bargaining power to negotiate visa-waiver agreements with the EU.

05
For International Organisations
Create a Global Mobility Fairness Index

Develop a metric measuring reciprocity, visa approval rates, and fee equity, exposing countries that benefit from an open world while maintaining closed borders.

Toward a Fairer Global Mobility Regime

The global mobility divide is rooted in colonial history and sustained by a visa regime that is both discriminatory and strategically short-sighted. Reform is not only just. It is also pragmatic for the EU. With Africa expected to account for around 25% of the world’s working-age population by 2050, states that build trust and expand mobility now will be better placed to benefit from future partnership, talent, and influence.

The contradiction is stark: European states that once extracted wealth from Africa now impose costly visa barriers on African applicants. The task is to build a fairer mobility regime, one that treats movement less as a privilege of birth and more as a shared global right.
